## Why Try It?

Reading about accessibility barriers is one thing. Experiencing them — even briefly — changes how you think about the products you build. This page walks you through two quick exercises you can do right now, on this tutorial site.

No special tools required. Just your keyboard, your browser, and your words.

## Exercise 1: Keyboard Navigation

Put your mouse aside. For the next few minutes, navigate this site using only your keyboard.

**The basics:**

- **Tab** — move to the next interactive element (link, button, input)
- **Shift + Tab** — move to the previous interactive element
- **Enter** — activate a link or button
- **Space** — activate a button or toggle a checkbox
- **Arrow keys** — move within menus, radio groups, or tabs
- **Escape** — close a modal or dismiss a popup

**Things to try:**

1. Starting from this page, use **Tab** to move through the sidebar navigation. Can you tell where you are? Is the focus indicator visible?
2. **Tab** to the header. Can you reach the "Tutorial" and "Demo" links?
3. Navigate to the [People & Assistive Devices](/tutorial/people-and-devices) page using only the keyboard. How many Tab presses does it take?
4. Try using **Shift + Tab** to go backward. Does the order feel natural?
5. Navigate to the [Maple Valley Health demo](/maple-valley-health) and try completing a task — find a service, reach the contact form. Can you do it without touching your mouse?

**What to notice:**

- Can you always see where the focus is?
- Are there places where focus seems to disappear or jump unexpectedly?
- Do any elements feel unreachable?
- Is the order logical, or do you find yourself Tabbing through things that don't match the visual layout?

## Exercise 2: Zoom

Now bring your mouse back — but zoom your browser to 200%.

**How to zoom:**

- **macOS:** Cmd + Plus (+) to zoom in, Cmd + Minus (-) to zoom out, Cmd + 0 to reset
- **Windows/Linux:** Ctrl + Plus (+) to zoom in, Ctrl + Minus (-) to zoom out, Ctrl + 0 to reset

**Things to try:**

1. Zoom to **200%** and browse this page. Does the layout adapt? Can you still read everything without scrolling horizontally?
2. Try the sidebar navigation at 200%. Is it still usable?
3. Zoom to **300%** or **400%**. At what point does the layout start to struggle?
4. Visit the [Maple Valley Health demo](/maple-valley-health) at 200%. Try reading the service descriptions and using the contact form.

**What to notice:**

- Does text reflow into a single column, or do you need to scroll sideways?
- Do any elements overlap or get cut off?
- Are buttons and links still easy to tap or click?
- Can you still read all the text, or does some of it get hidden?

## Exercise 3: Describe What You See

Screen reader users can't see images or icons. They rely entirely on the text descriptions that developers and content authors provide. This exercise helps you practice thinking about what information an image actually conveys — and what can be left out.

### Part A: Icons

Look at the icons below. For each one, imagine you can't see it — you can only hear a description read aloud. What would you need to hear to understand what the icon means?

- A magnifying glass icon in a website header
- A heart icon on a product listing
- A hamburger menu icon (three horizontal lines)
- An "X" icon in the corner of a dialog
- A red circle with an exclamation mark next to a form field

**Things to think about:**

- Does the icon represent an **action** (like "Search" or "Close") or a **status** (like "Error" or "Favorite")?
- Would the shape of the icon ("magnifying glass") help a screen reader user, or would the **purpose** ("Search") be more useful?
- If the icon sits next to visible text that already explains it, does it need its own description at all?

The key insight: icon descriptions should communicate **purpose**, not appearance. "Close dialog" is more useful than "X." "Search" is more useful than "magnifying glass." And if a label already says "Search" next to the icon, the icon itself can be hidden from screen readers entirely.

### Part B: Describing a Photo

Look at the image above. Now imagine you're writing the alt text — the description that a screen reader will announce in place of the image.

**Try writing two versions:**

1. **A short description** (one sentence) — what would you say if you were telling someone about this image in passing?
2. **A detailed description** — what would you say if the image was the main content of the page and the reader needed to understand it fully?

**Things to think about:**

- What is the **key information** in this image? What's the main subject?
- What details are **interesting but secondary**? Would a screen reader user miss anything important if those were left out?
- Does the **context** where the image appears change what you'd describe? The same photo in a news article, a photography portfolio, and a tutorial about alt text might each need a different description.
- Is there information in the image that **isn't visible** — like who the people are, where it was taken, or when? Should that be in the alt text, a caption, or both?

There's no single right answer. Alt text is a judgment call that depends on context. The goal is to give screen reader users the same **understanding** that sighted users get — not necessarily the same level of visual detail.

**Common mistakes to avoid:**

- Starting with "Image of..." or "Picture of..." — the screen reader already announces that it's an image
- Describing every visual detail when only one or two matter
- Writing "decorative image" for an image that actually carries meaning
- Leaving alt text empty on an image that communicates important information

## What Did You Find?

If everything worked perfectly — great. That means someone put thought into keyboard access and responsive design. If you hit friction points, you just experienced what millions of people encounter every day.

The difference is: you can switch back. You can grab your mouse, reset your zoom, and move on. For many users, these aren't exercises — they're the only way to use the web.

Every friction point you noticed is something a designer could consider in a mockup, a developer could catch during implementation, a tester could flag in a review, or a product manager could prioritize in a backlog. That's why these quick checks belong to the whole team.

## Key Takeaways

- You don't need specialized tools to start understanding accessibility barriers — a keyboard, browser zoom, and your own words are enough.
- Keyboard navigation reveals issues with focus order, focus visibility, and unreachable elements.
- Zoom testing reveals issues with layout reflow, text overflow, and element overlap.
- Icon descriptions should communicate purpose, not appearance — "Close" beats "X icon."
- Alt text is a judgment call that depends on context. The same image may need different descriptions in different places.
- Brief hands-on experience builds more intuition than hours of reading specifications.
- The barriers you notice in five minutes are the same ones your users face every day.
