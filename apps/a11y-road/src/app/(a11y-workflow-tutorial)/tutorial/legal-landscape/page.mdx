## Why Law Matters for Product Teams

You don't need to be a lawyer to build accessible products. But understanding that accessibility has a legal dimension changes how organizations prioritize it. When accessibility is framed only as "the right thing to do," it competes with every other priority. When it's also a legal requirement, it gets budget, timelines, and executive attention.

This page gives you enough context to understand the landscape — not to practice law.

The key takeaway: WCAG didn't appear overnight. It's the product of three decades of work by accessibility advocates, researchers, and standards bodies. When laws reference "WCAG 2.2 Level AA," they're pointing to a mature, well-tested framework.

## Major Laws and Regulations

The following is a high-level overview of the most commonly referenced accessibility laws. This is not exhaustive — many countries and regions have their own legislation.

### United States

**Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA)**

The ADA is a broad civil rights law prohibiting discrimination against people with disabilities. While the original 1990 law didn't mention the web specifically, courts have increasingly interpreted it to cover websites and digital services.

In 2024, the Department of Justice published a final rule under Title II of the ADA, establishing specific requirements for web and mobile accessibility for state and local government entities. The rule references WCAG 2.1 Level AA as the technical standard.

**Section 508 of the Rehabilitation Act**

Section 508 applies to federal agencies and organizations that receive federal funding. It requires that electronic and information technology be accessible. The 2017 refresh of Section 508 adopted WCAG 2.0 Level AA as the baseline standard.

### European Union

**European Accessibility Act (EAA)**

The EAA is a directive that requires certain products and services — including e-commerce, banking, and transportation — to be accessible. EU member states were required to transpose it into national law by June 2025. The EAA references the European standard EN 301 549, which in turn incorporates WCAG.

**Web Accessibility Directive (WAD)**

The WAD, adopted in 2016, requires public sector websites and mobile applications in the EU to meet accessibility standards based on EN 301 549.

### Other Regions

Many other countries have accessibility legislation, including Canada (Accessible Canada Act), the United Kingdom (Equality Act 2010), Australia (Disability Discrimination Act), and others. The specifics vary, but the trend is consistent: WCAG Level AA is the most widely referenced technical standard globally.

## The Common Thread

Despite the differences in scope and jurisdiction, almost every accessibility law points to the same technical benchmark: **WCAG Level AA**. The specific version varies — some reference 2.0, others 2.1, and the most recent regulations point to 2.2 — but the framework is the same.

This is why the previous pages in this section focused on WCAG. If you understand the principles, know the most common failures, and can map criteria to the people they serve, you're building on the same foundation that the legal landscape requires.

## Beyond Compliance

Laws set a floor, not a ceiling. Meeting WCAG Level AA is a meaningful achievement, but accessibility doesn't stop at conformance. Real accessibility means:

- Including people with disabilities in your research and testing
- Maintaining accessibility over time, not just achieving it once
- Responding to user feedback when barriers are reported
- Treating accessibility as a quality attribute, not a legal checkbox

## Key Takeaways

- Web accessibility has legal requirements in many jurisdictions. The specifics vary, but the trend is global.
- WCAG Level AA is the most widely referenced technical standard across laws and regulations.
- The ADA, Section 508, the European Accessibility Act, and the Web Accessibility Directive are among the most commonly cited laws.
- WCAG has been developed over three decades and is recognized as an ISO international standard.
- Laws set a minimum standard. Good accessibility practice goes beyond compliance.
- This overview is for educational purposes — consult a qualified attorney for guidance specific to your situation.
